Mix It Up With Hola —
Building a community-first architecture where peer content drives commercial conversion.

A structural redesign that closed the gap between a thriving community and an underperforming commerce layer — integrating purchase directly into the social experience rather than asking users to leave it.

01 — Problem

The most powerful social proof engine in the category — and a 1.2% conversion rate.

Mix It Up With Hola is a cocktail and mixology brand built around community creativity — 150,000 registered users discovering, remixing, and sharing cocktail recipes. The community was thriving: 4,000+ monthly recipe uploads, high engagement rates, a genuinely active creative ecosystem. By almost any community metric, the platform was succeeding.

The commerce layer told a different story. The business sells the spirits, mixers, and equipment featured in community content — but conversion from community pages to product purchase was 1.2%. The brand had built one of the most powerful authentic endorsement mechanisms available to a drinks brand — real people, making real drinks, sharing real results — and was monetising almost none of it.

The diagnostic was architectural. The path from inspiration — engaging with a recipe post featuring a specific bottle of amaro — to transaction — purchasing that bottle — required a user to leave the social context, navigate manually to a separate shop section, identify the specific product shown in the content, and complete a standard checkout. Each step was a conversion leak. The community was generating commercial intent at scale; the platform was systematically dissolving it through disjointed navigation.

02 — Strategic Thinking

The gap between inspiration and purchase was an architecture problem.

The design insight was structural: the community was generating enormous commercial intent, but the path from inspiration to transaction required users to leave the social context entirely. Contextual commerce — purchasing integrated directly into the content experience, with zero navigation required — was the solution. Every ingredient mentioned in community content would become a purchasable item, accessible in-place.

The second insight was about creator economics. The community was uploading 4,000 recipes a month without any commercial reward for that creative contribution. A creator commission model — where recipe authors earn on purchases generated by their content — would simultaneously increase creator incentive (more content) and increase content quality (creators invested in the commercial performance of their posts). The design and the incentive structure needed to be designed together.

Principle 01

Commerce in Context

Every ingredient mentioned in community content becomes a purchasable item, in-flow. The purchase path is measured in taps, not navigations. Context is not broken; it is deepened by the availability of the thing the user is already interested in.

Principle 02

Community Content as PDP

The most authentic product description is a user showing you what they made with it. Community content — photos, reviews, remixes — becomes the primary product page content. Brand claims recede; peer demonstration leads.

Principle 03

Social Proof at the Decision Point

The path to purchase shows peer activity — saves, makes, reviews — rather than brand claims. The evidence that others have bought and loved a product is more persuasive than any copy. Social proof belongs at the moment of commercial decision, not in a separate reviews section.

03 — UX Design Approach

Collapsing the distance between content and commerce.

The UX challenge was one of integration without intrusion. Community platforms fail commercially when the commerce layer feels imposed — when advertising or shopping mechanisms break the social experience rather than enriching it. The design requirement was that every commercial touchpoint had to feel as natural and value-additive as the community content surrounding it.

The product detail page was reimagined entirely. Rather than a brand-authored page with a reviews section appended, the PDP was restructured to lead with community content — the recipe posts, images, and reviews from the 150,000-strong user base — with product specifications, pricing, and the add-to-cart mechanism integrated into that community context. A user arriving at a product page would first see what the community had done with it, then decide whether to buy it. The sequence matched natural purchase psychology: social proof before commercial decision.

The "Make It Now" basket builder — assembling a complete shopping basket from all ingredients in a single recipe with one tap — was designed to solve for the specific purchase context of cocktail making. A user who wants to make a Negroni is not shopping for a bottle of gin; they are shopping for everything needed to make a Negroni. Matching the commerce unit to the natural unit of desire (the recipe) rather than the inventory unit (the SKU) dramatically increased average basket size.

04 — UI Design System

A visual system that makes community and commerce feel native to each other.

The design system was built to solve a specific tension: community platforms and commerce platforms have different visual registers, and the wrong combination feels either sterile (commerce colonising community) or untrustworthy (community aesthetics in a transactional context). The approach was to establish a single design language — expressive, high-energy, personality-led — and apply it consistently across both contexts, so that the move from a recipe post to a product page felt like a continuation, not a mode switch.

05 — Key Features & Solutions

Where community investment became commercial infrastructure.

Contextual Ingredient Linking

Every ingredient named in a recipe post links directly to the purchasable product, in-place — without navigating away from the recipe content. A user reading a recipe can tap any ingredient to see the product card, price, and add-to-cart, and return to the recipe without losing their place. The path from inspiration to purchase reduced from five steps to one.

Creator Commerce Integration

Recipe authors earn commission on purchases generated from their content — tracked transparently through a creator dashboard. The economic incentive is visible: creators see exactly which posts are driving revenue. The result was a 180% increase in content submissions within 8 weeks of launch, as creators invested in the quality and completeness of their recipe posts with commercial intent.

Community PDP Module

Product detail pages restructured to lead with user-generated content — recipe posts, photographs, reviews, and remixes from the community — with product specification and purchase information integrated into that social context. Brand claims replaced by peer demonstration. Products with community PDPs converted at 2.6× the rate of standard product pages.

"Make It Now" Basket Builder

One-tap basket assembly from a complete recipe ingredient list — adding every purchasable item from a recipe to the cart simultaneously. The commerce unit is the recipe, not the SKU, matching the natural unit of desire for a cocktail community. Average order value from basket builder purchases was £22 above the site average, because users were buying for an occasion rather than a single item.

Social Activity Feed on PDPs

A live feed of community members who have purchased and used a product — showing what they made, their rating, and a link to their recipe — displayed on the product page. Social proof at the precise moment of commercial decision, drawing on the community's existing activity without requiring additional content creation. PDPs with active social feeds converted at 1.8× those without.

Remix and Save Architecture

Community engagement tools — save, remix, comment — built into the commerce flow rather than separate from it. A user who saves a recipe is automatically offered the option to add its ingredients to a shopping list. A user who remixes a recipe is shown the ingredient differences and their purchasability. Engagement and commerce are not competing contexts; they are sequential stages of the same experience.

06 — Interaction & Motion

Motion that makes commerce feel social.

The motion design challenge on this project was distinctive: making commercial interactions feel as fluid and natural as social ones. The design principle was that a purchase action should feel like any other community action — a save, a remix, a share. The visual and motion language of adding to cart was designed to match the visual language of community engagement: the same weight, the same speed, the same sense of things settling into place.

The "Make It Now" basket builder uses a cascade animation — ingredients appearing one by one in the basket preview as the single-tap action assembles the order — giving the user a sense of watching their shopping list build itself. The sequence reinforces the magic of the feature: one decision, many items, all resolved. The animation pacing was calibrated against the natural reading speed of a cocktail recipe, ensuring the cascade felt responsive rather than slow.

The contextual ingredient link uses an in-place expansion — the product card slides up beneath the ingredient name, without navigating away from the recipe — maintaining the social context while adding a commercial layer. The expansion and collapse animation is spring-based, giving the interaction a physical quality that reinforces the sense that a real product is present in the content, not linked-to from it.

07 — Impact & Outcome

Community intent, commercially realised.

+180% User-generated content submissions (8 weeks post-launch)
+65% Conversion rate from community pages (1.2% to 2.0%)
+£22 Average order value from recipe basket builder
40% Of purchases now originating from community content pages

The creator economy integration produced a virtuous cycle that was anticipated in theory but exceeded expectations in practice. Creators who could earn from their content invested more in quality — longer recipes, better photographs, more precise ingredient specifications. Better content drove higher engagement. Higher engagement drove higher conversion. Higher conversion generated more creator earnings. The flywheel dynamics were built into the architecture; the scale at which they operated within eight weeks of launch validated both the model and the implementation.

08 — Reflection

What this project taught.

What worked

The contextual ingredient linking was the single highest-leverage feature — technically straightforward but architecturally transformative. Eliminating the navigation requirement between content and commerce proved to be the most effective conversion mechanism available. The lesson: conversion optimisation is often not a checkout problem or a product page problem. It is a navigation problem. Removing steps produces more impact than optimising the steps that remain.

What would evolve

The creator dashboard was delivered as a read-only analytics view in the initial release. A future iteration would introduce creator tooling — the ability to curate ingredient links, optimise for conversion, and see real-time engagement data — turning creators into active commercial partners rather than passive content suppliers. The economic model strongly supports this investment: high-performing creators drive disproportionate revenue.

Trade-offs made

A decision was made to present the creator commission model as a visible, celebrated feature of the platform — not a behind-the-scenes arrangement. This required negotiating with the brand team, who were concerned about the platform appearing commercially motivated. The counter-argument was that transparency about creator economics is a trust signal for the community: it explains why the content is high quality, and why creators care about the accuracy of what they post. The community responded positively to the transparency.

Design leadership dimension

This project required holding two simultaneous design briefs in alignment: one for the community experience and one for the commerce experience. The failure mode — which most social commerce products exhibit — is optimising for one at the expense of the other. The design leadership challenge was maintaining the community product's integrity while integrating commercial functionality that could easily have felt extractive. That balance required continuous advocacy throughout the project, and continuous reference back to the user research that established what the community valued most.

"We always knew the community was valuable — we just couldn't prove it commercially. Now our best-performing product pages are ones a member wrote the content for. That's the platform working as it should."
— CEO, Mix It Up With Hola
