The Getaway —
An arrival-first design strategy: where emotional connection precedes commercial transaction.

Reimagining the digital experience for a curated boutique travel brand — inverting the conventional platform sequence to lead with story, not search, and with place, not price.

Role

Senior Product Designer (UX + UI)

Scope

Product Strategy, UX Architecture, UI Design, Booking Experience

Platform

Web · Mobile · Responsive

Year

2024

Client

The Getaway

Outcome

+52% Booking Completion

01 — Problem

A curation brand communicating like a comparison engine.

The Getaway curates boutique accommodation across 12 countries — properties with genuine character, handpicked by a team of experienced travellers who have stayed in every one. The challenge was digital: the existing booking platform communicated like an OTA (rates, availability, filters) when the brand's true value was curation, knowledge, and the promise of an extraordinary stay.

Users arriving from editorial and social channels — where the brand had built significant awareness and genuine affection — landed on a product experience that felt transactional. The brand voice that had built the audience disappeared at the homepage. The bounce rate on the properties listing page was 62%. Of users who continued past the listing page, the average time before a booking attempt was 14 minutes — evidence that users needed far longer than the product warranted to feel confident enough to act.

The diagnostic reading was clear: the platform was asking users to make a significant purchase decision before it had done any of the work required to earn that decision. The information needed to want a specific property was locked in property descriptions that users had to navigate to independently — there was no design-led path from discovery to desire to decision.

02 — Strategic Thinking

Inverting the sequence: story first, availability second.

Most travel platforms present destination first, then story. The Getaway's redesign inverted this. Story first. Arrival second. Transaction third. The strategic reframe centred on sequence: the platform's job is not to display inventory — it is to make a user want a specific place enough that the availability check is an act of hope rather than an act of research.

The model drew from the editorial travel magazine — publications where the reader is transported to a place before they are offered the chance to go. The design challenge was to create that transportive quality in an interactive digital product, without losing the commercial efficiency that makes a booking platform viable. The answer was sequential design: each phase of the experience serves a specific emotional and commercial purpose, in a specific order.

Principle 01

Arrival Before Action

Immersive destination storytelling precedes any display of rates or availability. The user must feel the place before they are asked to book it. Commercial intent is earned through experience, not presented at the door.

Principle 02

Curation as Feature

The curator's editorial voice integrated directly into the property experience — not siloed to a magazine section. The team's knowledge is the product; the design makes it impossible to miss.

Principle 03

Friction as Filter

Certain premium touchpoints intentionally add deliberation time. The booking flow is designed for considered decisions, not impulse purchases — because an aligned guest is a loyal guest.

03 — UX Design Approach

Designing from arrival backward to transaction.

The user journey was redesigned starting from the emotional destination — what does it feel like to have found the right property? — and working backward to identify what each preceding design moment needed to accomplish. This inverted the conventional product design sequence, which typically starts with information architecture and works toward user experience. Here, experience was the architecture.

Occasion-led categorisation replaced destination-led browsing as the primary discovery path. Research with The Getaway's existing customers identified the most common booking motivations — honeymoon, remote retreat, family adventure, group celebration — and these became the primary navigation layer. Users arriving with an occasion in mind could find relevant properties within two clicks, compared to the five-click minimum required under the previous taxonomy.

The booking widget was deliberately positioned after the editorial content block on property pages — a design decision that required internal advocacy. The commercial instinct was to maximise visibility of the booking mechanism. The user research showed clearly that users who encountered the booking widget before understanding the property produced significantly lower completion rates. Sequencing the transaction correctly, after the emotional investment had been made, produced the opposite effect.

04 — UI Design System

A visual language built for immersion before transaction.

The design system was calibrated for one primary quality: the feeling of being somewhere extraordinary. Full-bleed imagery at every hero moment; generous typographic scale; a near-absence of interface chrome in the first 60vh of every property page. The UI recedes to allow the place to lead. Navigation, filtering, and booking UI emerge only once the user has had sufficient time to arrive.

05 — Key Features & Solutions

Moments that earn the booking.

Cinematic Property Hero

Full-bleed immersive video and editorial photography with no UI chrome for the first 60vh of every property page. The guest arrives at the property before they encounter any booking mechanism — the same sequence as an editorial feature, deliberately reproduced in a commercial context. Scroll depth beyond 60vh increased by 78% over the previous design.

Curator's Notes on PDPs

First-person editorial notes from the team member who visited each property — integrated directly into the property page, not siloed to a blog section. The voice of a trusted expert, speaking to a specific place at a specific time. Users who engaged with curator notes converted at 2.3× the rate of those who did not.

"Best For" Occasion Tags

Occasion-led property categorisation — honeymoon, remote work, family, adventure, group celebration — used as the primary discovery layer. Users arrive with a context, not a destination; the navigation meets them there. Time-to-relevant-property reduced from 5 clicks to 2 under the new architecture.

Transparent All-In Pricing

Pricing displayed as a complete figure inclusive of taxes, service charges, and any included experiences — with a clear breakdown available on expansion. The discovery that a quoted price does not reflect the actual cost is one of the leading causes of checkout abandonment in travel. Eliminating that moment of doubt removed a significant conversion barrier.

Availability-Secondary Layout

The booking widget deliberately positioned below the editorial content block on property pages. The sequence matters: emotional investment earned through editorial, commercial action invited after that investment is made. Testing showed booking widget visibility below the fold produced higher completion rates than above-fold placement.

Mobile Itinerary Planner

A save, compare, and share tool allowing users to build a shortlist of properties across multiple destinations — designed to support the realistic travel decision process, which involves multiple options considered over multiple sessions. Return visit rate among planner users was 3.2× that of non-planner users.

06 — Interaction & Motion

Motion that creates arrival, not navigation.

The motion system was designed around a single quality: the feeling of arriving somewhere. Property hero videos play at a deliberately slow pace — unhurried panning shots that communicate stillness rather than excitement. The transition from the listing page to a property page uses a full-screen expand animation — the selected property image growing to fill the viewport — creating a physical sense of entering a place.

The curator's notes section uses a slow fade-in reveal keyed to scroll position — the editorial voice appearing as the user descends into the property experience, arriving with them at the moment they are ready to receive it. Interaction timing throughout was calibrated against a reference set of luxury editorial experiences — the New York Times Travel section, Condé Nast Traveller — rather than against the travel booking platform category, where speed is the dominant design value.

The itinerary planner uses a gentle card-stack assembly animation — each saved property appearing to settle into a growing collection — reinforcing the feeling that the user is building something considered and personal, not completing a form. The save action is designed to feel like a deliberate decision, not an administrative task.

07 — Impact & Outcome

Story, converted.

+52% Booking completion rate (16-week post-launch)
+27% Direct booking rate (reduced OTA dependency)
14→8 min Average session-to-booking time
+41% Return visitor rate

The qualitative outcome was as significant as the quantitative. The Getaway reported a measurable improvement in guest quality post-launch — guests arriving more engaged, more aligned with the brand's values, and more likely to leave detailed reviews. The site was doing curatorial work that had previously relied entirely on the human team. That represented a significant operational leverage, not just a commercial one.

08 — Reflection

What this project taught.

What worked

The arrival-first sequence was the defining strategic insight of the project. The decision to invert the conventional platform architecture — story before availability, emotion before transaction — produced results that validated the approach comprehensively. The curator's notes integration in particular proved more commercially impactful than anticipated: a user who has read a first-person account of a place converts faster and with fewer questions than one who has only seen photographs.

What would evolve

The itinerary planner was released as a client-side experience without account persistence — saved properties were lost on session end. A future iteration with authenticated state would allow users to build and return to their shortlist across sessions and devices, significantly improving the experience for the multi-week travel decision process. The data on planner usage strongly justifies that investment.

Trade-offs made

The decision to position the booking widget below-the-fold required sustained advocacy against commercial intuition. The argument for above-the-fold placement — maximum visibility for the conversion mechanism — is understandable but incorrect in this context. The evidence from testing was unambiguous. Managing that tension productively — acknowledging the concern, presenting the evidence, and building agreement — was as demanding as any design decision on the project.

Design leadership dimension

This project required establishing a design philosophy that ran counter to the category convention. Travel platforms optimise for search speed and filter efficiency. The strategic argument was that The Getaway should not compete on those dimensions — because it cannot win there, and because its real competitive advantage lives elsewhere entirely. Establishing that positioning required confidence in the research, and the willingness to defend a direction that felt counterintuitive commercially until the data arrived.

"Our bookings increased significantly, but the thing that surprised us most was the quality of guest who arrived. People were more prepared, more engaged, more aligned with what we offer. The site is now setting expectations rather than just taking orders."
— Founder, The Getaway
