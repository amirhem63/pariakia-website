Marex —
Enterprise Trading Platform

Re-designing a complex financial trading platform to improve decision-making speed, reduce cognitive load, and deliver a best-in-class experience for professional traders.

–42% Task Completion Time
+68% User Satisfaction
0 Critical Errors in Testing
2025 Year

Marex

Financial Services

Services

  • UX Research

Duration

18 weeks

01 — Challenge

High-stakes decisions, high cognitive load.

Marex is a global financial services platform where traders make complex, time-sensitive decisions under significant pressure. The existing platform had evolved through years of incremental development — functional, but cluttered. Information was dense, hierarchies were unclear, and the interaction model created friction exactly where speed mattered most.

The redesign required deep domain expertise: understanding how professional traders think, what information they need at each moment in their workflow, and how to present complexity without creating cognitive overload.

02 — Approach

Embedded research with expert traders.

The project began with 3 weeks of embedded research — shadowing traders in their daily workflows, conducting cognitive task analysis, and running contextual inquiry sessions to understand not just what traders did, but how they thought while doing it. This revealed that the existing interface violated several fundamental principles of professional tool design: too much information presented simultaneously, poor data hierarchy, and interaction patterns that required unnecessary context switching.

The redesign introduced a clear information hierarchy based on decision priority, keyboard-first interaction patterns for high-frequency actions, and a configurable dashboard that allowed traders to personalise their workspace to match their specific role and trading style.

03 — Results

Speed, clarity, and confidence.

Moderated usability testing showed a 42% reduction in time to complete core trading tasks. User satisfaction scores — measured through a standardised SUS questionnaire — increased from 58 to 85. In 12 rounds of testing covering 47 critical tasks, zero critical errors were recorded in the final design iteration.
